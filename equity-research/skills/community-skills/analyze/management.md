# Management Assessment

description: Scores management quality across five measurable dimensions — capital-allocation track record (buyback price-weighted history, M&A value-creation, R&D-to-revenue conversion), executive compensation alignment with long-run shareholder value, insider transaction patterns over time, executive turnover signals, and tenure/background fit for the current business challenges. Use when initiating coverage, after a CEO or CFO transition, when capital allocation seems to be shifting, or before sizing a position in a name where management quality is the thesis. Triggers on "management quality", "capital allocation track record", "is [ticker]'s CEO compensation aligned", "insider buying / selling at [ticker]", "executive change at [ticker]", "is the new CEO a good fit", "compare management of [A] and [B]".

Capital allocation is the CEO's job. Most investor letters say this. Few investors actually check.

The track record is in the numbers: did they buy back stock at sensible prices or at peaks? Did acquisitions create value or destroy it? Is R&D spend translating into revenue, or is it a black box? Is executive compensation structured to align with long-run shareholder value, or does it reward revenue growth regardless of returns? These are not soft judgments — they are measurable patterns in the financial history.

This skill builds the structured assessment around five dimensions and produces a track-record verdict.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Pull inputs

For the named ticker:
- **5–10 years of capital-allocation history** — buyback $ and average price by year, M&A deal $ and outcomes, R&D spend, dividend history, debt issuance and repayment.
- **Most recent 3 proxy statements** — CEO and NEO compensation structure, performance metrics, target ranges, long-term equity grants.
- **Insider transactions, last 36 months** — open-market buys and sells by officers, directors, 10%+ holders.
- **Executive-change history, last 5 years** — CEO, CFO, COO, divisional heads, board departures.
- **Returns history** — total stock return over 1y, 3y, 5y, 10y vs. peer group and broad market.

### Step 2: Score the five management dimensions

| Dimension | What to compute | Strong signal | Weak signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Capital allocation — buybacks** | $ repurchased by year vs. average share price that year. % of buybacks done at ≤ 12-month-trailing-average price. Net share-count change despite SBC. | Buybacks concentrated in low-price years; net share count down despite SBC | Buybacks concentrated at all-time-high prices; net share count flat or up despite buyback program |
| **Capital allocation — M&A** | Outcomes of past deals: subsequent revenue trajectory of acquired businesses, goodwill impairments, asset write-downs, divestitures | Acquired businesses tracked separately and tracking deal-time expectations | Goodwill impairments; divestitures of prior acquisitions; quietly-dropped deal targets |
| **Capital allocation — R&D / capex efficiency** | Cumulative R&D ÷ cumulative incremental revenue over 5 years. Capex / revenue trend. | Each $ of R&D producing > $1 of incremental annual revenue within 3 years; capex tied to identified unit-economic improvement | R&D rising while revenue growth decelerates; capex rising without revenue or unit-economic justification |
| **Compensation alignment** | NEO comp structure: % long-term equity vs. cash bonus, performance metrics used, target vs. realized payout history, dilution from grants | Long-term equity > 60% of NEO comp; metrics include returns (ROIC, FCF/share, TSR vs. peers), not just revenue; grant size proportional to performance | Bonus-dominant; metrics revenue-only or short-cycle; mega-grant resets after underperformance |
| **Insider behavior pattern** | Net insider $ flow over rolling 12m windows; cluster buying / selling; CEO and CFO position changes | CEO/CFO consistently net buyers on weakness; multi-insider open-market buys in a window | CFO unloads > 50% of holdings in 2 quarters; multiple insiders selling into highs; founder selling > 10% annually |

Each dimension gets a verdict: **Strong / Mixed / Weak / Concerning**, with specific dollar / percentage / date evidence.

### Step 3: Track executive changes

List every CEO, CFO, COO, divisional-head, and board departure in the last 5 years. Flag:
- Mid-year CFO departures
- Departures without a named successor (interim arrangements)
- Multiple resignations from the same function within 24 months
- Founder stepping back "to focus on product" or "to chair"
- Compensation-committee chair changes that coincide with comp-structure changes

### Step 4: Present

Open with the overall **management verdict**: Strong allocators / Capable operators with watchlist items / Mixed / Concerning. Then:
- The 5-dimension scoring table with the underlying numbers
- The executive-change timeline with the flagged items
- Side-by-side total shareholder return vs. peer group and broad market for the same period
- 3 things to watch on the next proxy, the next earnings call, and the next insider filing

## Important Notes

- **Buyback timing tells you more than buyback size.** A CEO who buys back $5B at the lows is not the same as one who buys back $5B at the highs. Always price-weight the analysis.
- **Goodwill impairments are the M&A track record.** A company that has never written down an acquisition either does very few deals — or hides the losses inside organic growth. Both are findings.
- **Insider selling is mostly noise, except in clusters.** A single CEO sale on a 10b5-1 plan is rarely informative. Three officers selling into the same window without prior buybacks is.
- **Tenure cuts both ways.** A 25-year CEO can be a master compounder or an entrenched problem. Read tenure against the period's TSR and the underlying business trajectory.
- **Compensation structure is the leading indicator.** A new long-term-equity-heavy plan tied to ROIC tends to precede improved capital allocation. A plan refresh after a price drop tends to precede multiple compression.
- **Cite the proxy.** All compensation claims need exact proxy citations — they are the most contested findings the skill produces.
- **CEO transitions are inflection points, not data points.** A new CEO inherits a track record they did not write. The first 24 months — capital allocation, comp design, executive team changes — is where the new track record actually begins.
