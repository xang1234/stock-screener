# Business Model Analysis

description: Builds a structured, economist's view of how a company makes money and whether the model is durable. Scores eight dimensions — pricing power, customer concentration, industry structure, barriers to entry, cyclicality, capital intensity / operating leverage, AI disruption or opportunity, regulatory & geopolitical exposure — then tests for pivots the market hasn't yet articulated. Use when initiating coverage, before sizing a position, or when a re-rating defies headline numbers. Triggers on "business model", "moat", "how does [ticker] make money", "is [ticker]'s position widening or narrowing", "what protects [ticker] from competition", "has [ticker] pivoted", "AI risk to [ticker]", "pricing power", "customer concentration".

Before you look at a single number, understand how the company makes money — and why customers can't easily leave.

A great business has three qualities that compound over time: pricing power, switching costs, and a structural position in a growing market. Most companies have one of these. Few have all three. The financials are just the scoreboard — the business model is the game being played.

This skill builds the qualitative core: what the company sells, who buys it and why, where revenue is recurring versus transactional, what would happen if the largest customer left, and whether the competitive position is widening or narrowing. We use SEC filings and management commentary read critically, then score the model across eight economist's dimensions. Pivots — quiet shifts in revenue mix, pricing model, or customer overlap — often explain valuation re-ratings before the street has named them.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Frame the company

One paragraph of orientation: legal name, segments, geographies, scale (revenue, employees), CEO, recent corporate actions (spin, M&A, segment realignment). This is orientation, not analysis.

### Step 2: Read the disclosure base

In this order:
- **10-K Item 1 (Business).** What management says they do, segment by segment.
- **10-K Item 1A (Risk Factors).** Read what they fear — cyclicality, regulatory, concentration risks usually surface here before anywhere else.
- **MD&A.** Where the segment economics, demand drivers, and recent shifts live.
- **Segment & customer-concentration tables.** % of revenue by segment / geography / customer. Required disclosure for any customer > 10%.
- **Most recent earnings call.** How management currently frames the moat, the competitors, and the demand environment.

Read critically, not credulously. Management writes for narrative; your job is to find the gaps.

### Step 3: Decompose the business model in five lines

Write down, in five short lines, per segment if segments are materially different:

1. **What's sold** — product or service, specifically, not the marketing category.
2. **Who buys it** — buyer type, end-use, decision-maker.
3. **How they're reached** — direct sales / channel / platform / OEM / consumer.
4. **How it's priced** — subscription / license / usage / transactional / hardware-plus-service / take-rate.
5. **Recurring or transactional** — % of revenue that comes back every period without a new sales motion.

### Step 4: Score the eight durability dimensions

For each dimension, score Strong / Mixed / Weak / Negative and cite specific evidence:

| Dimension | The question | Strong signal | Weak signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pricing power** | Can prices rise in real terms without losing volume? | ASP trajectory > inflation; GPM stable/expanding through cycles | ASP flat/declining; GPM tracks input costs |
| **Customer concentration** | What happens if the top customer leaves? | Top 10 < 30% of revenue; multi-year contracts | One customer > 20%; no contractual lock-in |
| **Industry structure** | How concentrated is the industry? How rational is competition? | 3–5 players cover > 70% share; disciplined pricing | Fragmented; share-loss-driven pricing wars |
| **Barriers to entry** | What stops a credible new entrant? | Scale economics, network effects, switching costs, regulatory licenses, IP, branded trust | Capital alone; "great team"; first-mover |
| **Cyclicality** | What cycle is the business riding? | Tied to a secular grower (electrification, aging, data) with low macro beta | Tied to commodity prices, housing, broad capex cycle |
| **Capital intensity / op leverage** | How does cost per unit move with volume? | Low capex/sales, high incremental margin | High capex/sales, low incremental margin |
| **AI exposure** | Does AI substitute, augment, or expand the TAM? | Owns proprietary data; AI augments the workflow; sells into AI infra | Knowledge-worker substitute; undifferentiated content; vulnerable workflow layer |
| **Regulatory / geopolitical** | What does policy do to the business? | Stable, multi-jurisdiction; regulation favors incumbents | Single-jurisdiction; sensitive to tariff, export, or licensing change |

Each dimension gets a one-line verdict tied to specific evidence from Step 2's filings — not abstractions.

### Step 5: Test for pivot

Compare today's answers against the 10-K from 2–3 years ago. Has any dimension shifted? Common pivot signals:

- Revenue mix shifting from one segment to another (often masked inside headline growth)
- Customer concentration rising as a new large customer pulls revenue forward
- Pricing model migrating (license → subscription, hardware → service, transaction → take-rate)
- New segment launched that competes with existing customers (vertical integration tell)
- Capital intensity rising as the company moves up or down the value chain
- AI exposure shifting from "irrelevant" to "core" — or vice versa

A pivot that the street has not yet articulated is the highest-alpha finding this skill produces.

### Step 6: Present

Open with a one-line **durability verdict**: "Compounder" / "Quality cyclical" / "Strategic hold with watchlist items" / "Avoid until [X] resolves." Then:

- The five-line business-model summary (Step 3), per segment if applicable
- The eight-dimension scoring table (Step 4)
- The pivot read (Step 5) — what has changed and what it means
- **3 things to watch over the next 4 quarters** that would confirm or invalidate the verdict

## Important Notes

- **The financials are the scoreboard; the business model is the game.** Score the model first; the multiple should compress or expand to reflect it.
- **Read for what's missing.** A 10-K that doesn't disclose customer concentration when peers do is informative. Management's silence on AI in a sector where peers are loud is informative.
- **Pricing power shows up in gross margin, not press releases.** A company that genuinely has it doesn't need to talk about it.
- **Cyclical ≠ bad.** A great cyclical bought at the right point in the cycle outperforms a mediocre compounder. Score cyclicality to understand timing, not as a disqualifier.
- **AI exposure cuts both ways and quickly.** A business that looks AI-protected today (regulated, data-locked, workflow-embedded) can be re-rated in a single product cycle. Re-score this dimension every 12 months.
- **Watch the segment that's hiding.** A "10% of revenue" segment growing at 80% will be 40% in three years. Pivots usually live in the small fast-growing segment, not the headline number.
- **Customers competing with their suppliers is the canary.** When a key customer starts building what you sell, the moat is closing — flag immediately.
